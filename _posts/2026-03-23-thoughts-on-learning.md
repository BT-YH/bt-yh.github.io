Troubled thoughts of a beginning graduate students.


I am currently in my second semester of physics PhD program at Notre Dame, and it has not been what I expected. It has occurred to me that my undergraduate education was discouragingly insufficient. And the fault is the way I had approached learning physics. 

We often hear that doing physics is like playing a sport or an instrument, in the sense that daily practice is needed for mastery of the activity. The analogy often goes on by saying that one can't become a professional football player by just watching many football games or one can't become a professional pianist by just watching Langlang, therefore, one cannot obtain mastery in physics by merely reading and thinking, but by much, albeit mundane, practice. This is not to dismiss, however, the value of reading and thinking. 
Reading, thinking, and practicing, when applied in the right manner, guarantee effective learning. 

The trouble is that these three aspects of learning take a lot of time, and as students we are subjected to many deadlines that strip us of the freedom to indulge in thinking about matters slowly, so as to lay down a firm and secure foundation. A house built on sand cannot withstand rain and storm, and it's better to simply tear down the house and find a solid foundation. In the same way, I think the type of learning (reading, thinking, and practicing) that has not recieved careful thought because of time constraint, is building a house on the sand; important questions on the topic learned directed toward the person would easily reveal this shaky foundation, which has been my personal experience.

However, learning is iterative. So the foundation is gradually improved. But iteration, again, takes time. So doesn't it all just come down to a race against time? But I have wasted so much time. Frustration. Anxiety. Self-doubt. The worst is losing interest, and then no motivation. Learning is a luxury, and it should be an enjoyment, but with the external and internal pressure it becomes a chore. No one likes doing chores all the time. This is what I feel about physics sometimes. 

How do I get out of this? Am I seeking self-approval? Am I seeking others' approval? Am I really cut out for this? Am I making physics my idol? Will I fail?

I don't know if I am making physics my idol. I still think that mine is a naive and simple drive to learn more about this world, and to apply what I've learned to discover all the incredible things our mother nature or heavenly father has presented to us. What I am sure of is that **I dread failure**. Just the idea of failing makes me squirm in my chair as I'm typing this. Why do I dread failure so much? And what counts as failure? Not meeting the 3.0 GPA requirement. Okay that's a fair point. Getting a good grade isn't hard, it's easy to cheat the system: looking up solutions to homework problems, cramming for exams the night before... I hate doing these things. I might be prideful, but I think it's for the sake of my learning. Can I really build my house on sand and slowly swap the sand out for rocks? Probably. Is it really worth risking probation to give myself the ideal learning? Probably not.

There has to be a middle ground. Like my adivsor told me, I have to pick and choose. Choose what is the utmost important. Maintain my GPA above 3.0. 

I have to pick and choose. I can't doing everything perfectly. I have to mix some sand in the rocks.
