This is a summary of the [J. Robert Oppenheimer Memorial Prize Acceptance Speech by Paul A. M. Dirac.](https://www.goodreads.com/book/show/674804.Development_of_Quantum_Theory)



Dirac briefly discusses the early history of quantum theory from his perspective in a non-technical way. He started with a discussion of fears and hopes. The research worker is commonly thought of as unemotional, rational, and logical and when they are hot on the rail to make a major discovery, they can assess the situation calmly. Dirac claims this is far from truth. 

> “The research worker is only human and, if he has **great hopes**, he also has **great fears**.”

He gives the insight that the originator of an idea is not most fitted to develop it, because of the fear of the idea being falsified. This is why Einstein discovered Special Relativity instead of Lorentz, who had established almost all of the necessary mathematics. Dirac also gives example of himself and Heisenberg. Heisenberg focused on developing his theory centered on the idea of measurable quantities, and established non-commutativity as a secondary idea (a very foreign idea at the time and Heisenberg was fearful of this consequence). But Dirac, not being the originator of the idea, is not afraid of centering his theory on non-commutativity. They achieved the same results, but as we know today, non-commutativity is more fundamental to the quantum theory.

He gave another example: after Schrödinger developed his wave equation by generalizing De Broglie waves, he applied it the hydrogen spectra, but got inconsistent results with observations. He was so fearful and disappointed in his wave theory that he abandoned his theory for weeks before returning it and finding that if he removed the relativistic corrections his prediction is consistent with observations. So he published the result without relativistic corrections. But the “false”equation he discovered earlier was later rediscovered by Klein and Gordon and they published it despite the inconsistency, because they were not the originators of the “wave equation” framework and they have no fear of its collapse. Turned out this equation was inconsistent for hydrogen spectra observation because people didn’t know about spin at the time, and this equation does work for spin-less particles and it’s now called “Klein-Gordon” equation despite it being first discovered by Schrödinger a couple of year before.

Dirac gave a couple of further examples to illustrate his point. Then he changes his focus briefly on facing difficulties in the development of science:

> “when one gets over one difficulty, one is immediately faced with a newer difficulty and you might at first sight think that no real progress is made. But real progress is made because the new difficulty is more remote than the previous one. [The new difficulty was really not new and it was just hidden.]” 

This leads him to discuss the discovery of positron, which of course involved many great hopes and fears from various scientists. Dirac then comments that people back then were very reluctant to postulate a new particle and would only do so if all other possibilities were exhausted. This was why experimentalist at Cavendish failed to discover the positron and took the instances of positron generation to be coincidences of electron moving in the opposite direction. The present-day situation, says Dirac, is quite the opposite in that new particles are always being hypothesized.

Dirac concludes by noting that the theories developed as a consequence of these early ideas are “more or less patch work and do not have a fundamental basis”. He looks forward to another golden age of physics triggered by some drastic new idea leading to “a period of rapid development with great hopes and fears.


Dirac gives an intriguing perspective on the early history of quantum mechanics. His insights on the research process regarding the hopes and fears and resolving layers difficulties with never be obsolete.
